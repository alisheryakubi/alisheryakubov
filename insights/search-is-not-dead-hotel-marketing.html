Search Is Not Dead. Weak Hotel Marketing Is.

Travel discovery is fragmenting across Google, AI platforms, maps, social media and booking engines. The answer is not to abandon search. It is to build a brand that can be found, trusted and chosen wherever intent appears.

By Alisher Yakubov, Head of Marketing & Communications at Rotana and Hospitality Net World Panel Expert · Published July 21, 2026 · 7 min read · Hospitality Marketing

I recently joined 16 industry colleagues on the Hospitality Net Digital Marketing World Panel to discuss a question that is becoming impossible for hotel marketers to ignore: is traditional search marketing dead?

My answer was direct: search is not dead; weak marketing is.

The dramatic headlines make a good social post, but they make a poor business strategy. Travelers have not stopped searching. When money, time and an important experience are at stake, people still validate their decisions. They compare hotels, examine locations, read reviews, check rates, study room types and look for reasons to trust one property over another.

What has changed is where that journey begins and how many environments influence it.

A traveler may discover a destination on TikTok, ask ChatGPT to build an itinerary, use Google Maps to understand the location, read reviews on an OTA and then search the hotel's name before booking. That is not the death of search. It is the expansion of search.

For hotel brands, the strategic question is no longer, “Should we invest in Google or AI?” It is: Are we visible wherever guest intent exists?

Discovery is fragmenting, but decision-making still needs trust

AI is gaining influence at the top of the funnel, where people ask broad questions such as:

  • Where should I stay for a quiet beach holiday in the UAE?
  • Which hotels are suitable for a family weekend in Fujairah?
  • What is the best area for a wellness escape?

Social platforms influence the dreaming stage through visual proof, recommendations and stories. Maps shape local discovery. OTAs remain powerful comparison environments.

Yet the closer travelers move toward a booking, the more specific their behavior becomes. They search a property or brand by name. They compare the official rate with an OTA. They verify the location, recent reviews, dining options, room details and cancellation terms. They want reassurance that the experience will match the promise.

This creates a simple but important distinction:

  • Discovery is distributed. A hotel can enter the consideration set through social media, AI, PR, maps, an OTA or word of mouth.
  • Conversion is concentrated. A booking still depends on strong brand presence, credible information, rate confidence and a low-friction path to purchase.

A hotel that wins attention but cannot be verified loses trust. A hotel that appears in search but has never created demand competes mainly on price. Strong marketing connects both sides.

The three-layer visibility model for hotel brands

Hotels now need to manage three layers as one system.

1. Traditional search captures existing intent

Traditional search remains the most valuable environment for guests who are actively comparing or preparing to book. This includes organic search, branded paid search, Google Maps, Google Business Profile, Google Hotel Ads, metasearch and the hotel's own booking engine.

The immediate priorities are practical:

  • Protect branded searches so OTAs and affiliates do not become the easiest route to book your own hotel.
  • Maintain accurate names, locations, contact details, amenities, images and opening information everywhere the property appears.
  • Build useful pages around real traveler needs, not only generic destination keywords.
  • Keep rates and availability accurate and preserve a clear direct-booking value proposition.
  • Make the mobile journey from landing page to confirmed reservation fast and simple.
  • Treat reviews and management responses as conversion content, not a reputation task that sits outside marketing.

Traditional search does not only generate traffic. It validates the brand at the moment a traveler is deciding whether it deserves trust.

2. AI visibility builds consideration

AI answer engines are becoming a new layer of discovery. They do not simply present ten links; they assemble an answer and often mention only a limited number of properties, brands or sources.

That changes the competitive objective. Ranking is still useful, but being understandable and mention-worthy becomes equally important.

Hotels should give both people and machines clear evidence to work with:

  • Publish direct answers to real guest questions about the destination, location, rooms, dining, family stays, accessibility, wellness, meetings and local experiences.
  • Use specific facts and distinctive proof instead of interchangeable language such as “unforgettable luxury.”
  • Keep property facts consistent across the website, maps, social profiles, OTAs, directories and media coverage.
  • Structure pages clearly with descriptive headings, concise answers, original images, FAQs and internal links.
  • Add accurate Article, Person, Organization and relevant hospitality structured data that matches the visible page content.
  • Earn credible third-party mentions through PR, partnerships, awards, expert commentary and guest reviews.

There is no magic “AI optimization” switch. Google's own guidance says the fundamentals still matter for its AI features: useful people-first content, crawlable pages, sound internal linking, good page experience and structured data that agrees with what users can see.

AI visibility is therefore not a separate campaign. It is the result of a strong, consistent digital brand.

3. Social discovery creates future intent

Search is excellent at capturing a desire that already exists. Social media can create that desire earlier.

For hotels, this means moving beyond a calendar filled with room photographs and promotional posters. The strongest social content gives people a reason to remember and later search for the brand:

  • A first-person arrival experience
  • A one-day destination itinerary starting from the hotel
  • A chef, concierge or colleague explaining something only an insider would know
  • A family, wellness, dining or celebration story built around a real guest need
  • A clear visual signature repeated consistently enough to become recognizable

The goal is not to force every social interaction into an immediate booking. The goal is to create memory, preference and branded demand—then make sure search and the direct channel can capture it.

The “almost free marketing” opportunity

Max Starkov made a useful observation in a related Hospitality Net discussion about zero-click search: when a Google Ads campaign uses cost-per-click pricing, a brand may receive an impression without paying a click charge if the user does not click. In that limited sense, zero-click exposure can behave like free brand marketing.

The distinction matters. This visibility is not truly costless. Campaign setup, creative, technology, agency support and internal time still have a cost. An impression also has little value if the brand is not distinctive or if nobody remembers it. Hotels should treat no-click visibility as a brand benefit within a well-managed campaign—not as a reason to stop measuring performance.

There is also a more literal free opportunity: Google Free Booking Links. Google states that hotel partners pay no fee for placement or clicks on these links. They can take travelers from Google directly to the hotel's booking page, subject to eligibility, accurate rates, a suitable landing page and connectivity through Hotel Center or an integration partner.

Hotels should therefore audit four low-cost visibility assets:

  1. Google Business Profile: complete information, fresh imagery, review responses and accurate links.
  2. Google Free Booking Links: an official direct-booking option with accurate live rates and a strong landing experience.
  3. Useful owned content: destination guides, answers, itineraries and expert advice that can continue earning discovery after publication.
  4. Earned authority: PR coverage, partner mentions, interviews, panel contributions and reviews that reinforce the same brand story.

“Almost free” should never be confused with effortless. These channels save media cost only when the hotel invests in accuracy, content quality, reputation and conversion.

A practical 90-day hotel visibility plan

Days 1–30: Find the gaps

  • Search the hotel and brand names on Google, Maps, major OTAs, social platforms and several AI assistants.
  • Record inaccurate facts, weak imagery, missing links and inconsistent descriptions.
  • Check whether the official website owns the branded search experience.
  • Confirm whether Google Free Booking Links are active and accurately tracked.
  • Review the mobile booking path, rate parity, page speed and booking-engine friction.
  • Identify the 20 questions guests ask most often before booking.

Days 31–60: Build useful evidence

  • Publish four genuinely useful articles or guides answering high-value guest questions.
  • Improve core property and experience pages with specific details and original proof.
  • Create or strengthen the author and brand profile pages.
  • Add or validate Article, Person and Organization structured data.
  • Standardize facts and descriptions across priority platforms.
  • Turn the best guest questions into website FAQs, short videos and social posts.

Days 61–90: Distribute and measure

  • Convert each useful article into a short video, carousel, executive post, email and PR pitch.
  • Link every format back to the most relevant owned page instead of only to the homepage.
  • Track branded search growth, direct-booking revenue, assisted conversions, Free Booking Link performance, review sentiment and AI mentions.
  • Compare performance by audience and intent—not only by last-click attribution.
  • Update content when facts, offers or traveler questions change.

Measure the connected journey

LayerStrategic jobUseful indicators
Social discoveryCreate awareness and memoryQualified reach, saves, shares, video completion, branded-search lift
AI visibilityEnter and shape considerationBrand mentions, cited pages, referral quality, assisted conversions
Traditional searchCapture and validate intentSearch impression share, qualified traffic, booking-engine starts, direct revenue
Brand and reputationBuild confidence across every layerReview quality, share of voice, earned mentions, direct traffic

No single metric tells the full story. A social video may create the interest, AI may narrow the shortlist, Google may validate the property and the official website may close the booking. Judging every channel only by the final click encourages fragmented decisions—the very problem hotel marketers need to solve.

“Search did not die. It expanded. The hotels that win are the ones visible at every point where a guest's intent appears—from the first TikTok to the final direct booking.”

What hotels should do now

Stop asking whether to invest in Google, AI or social. Start asking whether your brand is visible, consistent and credible wherever guest intent appears. The three layers—search, AI, social—are not competing channels. They are one connected journey, and the hotel that treats them as one system will outperform the hotel that manages each in isolation.

The work is unglamorous: accurate listings, useful content, consistent facts, responsive reviews, structured data, original photography, fast booking. None of it is fashionable. All of it compounds. The hotels that do this well will not need to debate whether search is dead, because they will be found, trusted and chosen wherever travelers look.

Search is not dead. Weak marketing is. And the remedy is the same as it has always been: be useful, be consistent, be findable.
